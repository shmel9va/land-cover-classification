SLIDE 1 — Title slide                                          [~0:45]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Good morning, everyone. My name is Daniil Khrestyanovskiy, and together with my
co-author Mariia Shmeleva we are presenting our project "Land Cover Classification
from Satellite Imagery Using CNN and U-Net Architectures."

This work was carried out at Peter the Great St. Petersburg Polytechnic University,
at the Institute of Cybersecurity and Computer Science, under the supervision of
Mohamad Khalil.

Our goal was to train and compare two deep learning models that can automatically
determine what type of land cover appears in a satellite image — for example, whether
it is a forest, a city, a river, or a crop field. The dataset we used is called
EuroSAT, which is derived from the Sentinel-2 satellite mission. The models were
implemented in PyTorch.

Our talk will take around 20 to 25 minutes, after which we are happy to answer
any questions.

SLIDE 2 — Agenda                                               [~0:30]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Let me briefly walk through what we will cover today.

We will start with the motivation behind our work — why land cover classification
matters and where it is used. Then we will review existing approaches and explain
what limitations they have. After that, we will describe our proposed solution,
the EuroSAT dataset, and both neural network architectures in detail.

In the second half we will go through the training setup and hyperparameters,
present the numerical results for both models, and compare them head to head.
We will close with our conclusions and directions for future work.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 3 — Motivation                                           [~1:30]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Let us start with the motivation. Why do we need automatic land cover classification
at all?

The answer lies in the scale of modern urbanization and environmental monitoring.
Cities are expanding rapidly, and decision-makers — urban planners, environmental
agencies, and governments — need up-to-date maps of what is actually happening on
the ground. Is a forest being cleared? Is agricultural land being converted to
residential zones? Are water bodies shrinking?

Traditional approaches rely on human analysts who visually interpret satellite
images. This is accurate but extremely slow and does not scale. A single satellite
like Sentinel-2 covers the entire European continent every five days. The volume
of imagery produced in a week alone would take a team of analysts months to review
manually. So automation is not just convenient — it is the only practical option.

Land cover classification is therefore a key technology for smart city systems,
for environmental monitoring, for agricultural planning, and for disaster risk
assessment. In all of these domains, you need accurate, up-to-date maps — and you
need them fast.

Our work addresses this problem directly. We work with the EuroSAT dataset:
27,000 satellite image patches from Sentinel-2, each 64 by 64 pixels, spanning
10 different land cover types. Our task is to train models that can label each
patch with the correct class.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 4 — Project goal                                         [~1:00]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The goal of the project is formulated as follows: to design and compare deep learning
models capable of automatically classifying land cover types from Sentinel-2 imagery
with high accuracy.

To achieve this, we set three concrete objectives.

First — to build a baseline convolutional neural network, which we call SimpleCNN.
This gives us a reference point: a well-understood, fast model that we can use to
measure how much the second model actually improves things.

Second — to adapt the U-Net architecture for scene-level classification. U-Net was
originally designed for biomedical image segmentation, and we will explain exactly
how we repurposed it to output a class label for an entire image rather than a
pixel-wise map.

Third — to compare both models fairly on the same data split and the same set of
evaluation metrics, so that the comparison is meaningful.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 5 — Related work                                         [~1:30]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Our work builds on a solid body of existing research.

The EuroSAT dataset itself was introduced by Helber and colleagues in 2019. In their
original paper they showed that fine-tuned ResNet architectures can achieve more than
98% accuracy on this benchmark. This is an important reference point — it tells us
the upper bound of what is achievable with very deep, pre-trained models.

The U-Net architecture was proposed by Ronneberger, Fischer, and Brox in 2015 for
segmenting biomedical images. What made it special was the use of skip connections
between the encoder and the decoder, which allowed the network to recover fine
spatial details that are normally lost during downsampling. Since then, U-Net and
its variants have been widely adopted across remote sensing tasks.

Maggiori and colleagues demonstrated that fully convolutional networks are effective
for pixel-level labeling of aerial images. Nogueira carried out a systematic comparison
of CNN architectures for remote sensing scene classification and highlighted the
importance of transfer learning.

He and colleagues introduced ResNet in 2016, showing that very deep networks can be
trained effectively using residual connections. ResNet-based models are now the
standard baseline for image classification across many domains.

Finally, Li and colleagues in 2018 investigated attention mechanisms for remote sensing
scene classification, showing that directing the network's focus to the most
discriminative regions of an image further improves performance.

All of these works form the landscape against which our project is positioned.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 6 — Limitations and our contribution                     [~1:30]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Despite this progress, existing approaches have several important limitations that
motivated our work.

The first is the reliance on very deep networks pre-trained on ImageNet. While these
models are powerful, there is a well-known domain gap between natural photographs and
satellite imagery. Satellite images look very different — the viewing angle is top-down,
the textures are different, and the color statistics are very different from consumer
photos. Pre-training on ImageNet provides a head start, but it does not fully bridge
this gap.

The second limitation is that multispectral or hyperspectral methods, which can
deliver very high accuracy, require additional sensor data that is not always available.
Many real-world applications have access only to standard RGB imagery.

Third, most published benchmarks focus on pixel-level segmentation — predicting a
class label for every single pixel in an image. Our task is different: we want to
classify a whole image patch as belonging to one category. This is called scene-level
classification, and it is a distinct problem that has received less attention.

Finally, high computational cost and the black-box nature of many models are practical
barriers to deployment in production systems like smart cities.

Our approach addresses these limitations directly. We perform a systematic comparison
between a lightweight baseline CNN and a U-Net-based classifier. Crucially, we adapt
U-Net to scene-level classification by replacing the segmentation head with a global
average pooling layer and a fully connected classifier. Both models are trained entirely
from scratch — no ImageNet pre-training — and we use a fixed random seed to ensure
that every result is fully reproducible.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 7 — EuroSAT dataset                                      [~1:30]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Let me now describe the dataset in more detail.

EuroSAT is derived from the Sentinel-2 satellite constellation, which is part of the
European Copernicus Earth observation programme. Sentinel-2 provides multispectral
imagery at spatial resolutions ranging from 10 to 60 meters per pixel, with a revisit
time of five days. This makes it one of the most useful and widely used satellite
data sources for land cover analysis.

The full EuroSAT dataset contains 27,000 image patches. Each patch is 64 by 64 pixels
and corresponds to one of 10 land cover classes. The full dataset actually has 13
spectral bands, but in this work we use only the RGB version — the three visible
bands — because it is the standard benchmark and makes our results directly comparable
with related work.

Before feeding the images into the network, we normalize them using ImageNet statistics:
a mean of [0.485, 0.456, 0.406] and a standard deviation of [0.229, 0.224, 0.225]
per channel. This is a standard practice for RGB convolutional networks.

The 10 classes are: AnnualCrop, Forest, HerbaceousVegetation, Highway, Industrial,
Pasture, PermanentCrop, Residential, River, and SeaLake.

The class distribution is not perfectly balanced. Classes like Forest, AnnualCrop,
and Residential each have 3,000 samples, while Pasture has only 2,000 and several
others have 2,500. This mild imbalance is worth keeping in mind when interpreting
per-class metrics.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 8 — Data preparation                                     [~1:15]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

For training and evaluation, we split the dataset in a 70 / 15 / 15 ratio. That gives
us approximately 18,900 images for training, and 4,050 each for validation and test.

The split is performed using torchvision's ImageFolder class combined with
torch.random_split and a fixed random seed of 42. The use of a fixed seed is critical
here — it guarantees that whenever train.py or evaluate.py is run, they operate on
exactly the same partition of the data. This is what makes our results reproducible
across independent runs.

The roles of the three splits are strictly separated. The training set is used only
for gradient updates. The validation set is used only for one purpose: to select the
best epoch — the epoch at which the validation loss was lowest. The test set is never
seen during training or hyperparameter tuning; it is used exactly once, at the end,
to report the final metrics.

This separation is essential for preventing data leakage and ensures that the
performance numbers we report reflect genuine generalization to unseen examples.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 9 — SimpleCNN architecture                               [~1:30]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Now let us look at the first model — SimpleCNN.

As the name suggests, this is a deliberately simple, lightweight architecture.
It consists of three convolutional blocks followed by a fully connected classification
head.

Each convolutional block has the same structure: a 3 by 3 convolution with
padding of 1, which preserves the spatial size; followed by batch normalization;
a ReLU activation; and a 2 by 2 max-pooling that halves the spatial dimensions.

The number of feature channels grows progressively through the three blocks:
3 → 32 → 64 → 128. At the same time the spatial size shrinks:
64 × 64 → 32 × 32 → 16 × 16 → 8 × 8.

After the third pooling operation, the feature map is flattened into a vector
of 8,192 elements, which is 128 times 8 times 8. This vector is fed into the
first fully connected layer that maps it down to 512 neurons. We apply a Dropout
of 50% here to prevent overfitting. The second fully connected layer maps those
512 features to the 10 output classes.

In total, SimpleCNN has approximately 4.5 million trainable parameters and
occupies about 17 megabytes on disk. The vast majority of parameters — about
4.2 million — are in that first fully connected layer, which is why dropout
there is so important.

Batch Normalization proved to be critical in practice. In our preliminary
experiments, removing it reduced final accuracy by roughly 3 percentage points
and slowed down convergence noticeably.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 10 — U-Net Classifier architecture                       [~1:45]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The second model is the U-Net Classifier. Let me explain both the original U-Net
idea and what we changed.

The classical U-Net has two paths: an encoder, which progressively reduces the
spatial resolution while increasing the depth of the feature representation, and
a decoder, which does the opposite — it upsamples back toward the original resolution
while combining information from the encoder via skip connections.

In our encoder, the input image goes through four stages. Starting from 3 RGB
channels, we apply a DoubleConv block — two consecutive 3 by 3 convolutions, each
followed by batch normalization and ReLU — to produce 64 channels. Then we apply
max-pooling and repeat: 64 → 128, then 128 → 256, then 256 → 512. At this point
the spatial size has been reduced from 64 × 64 down to 8 × 8.

The decoder then upsamples using transposed convolutions. At each step, before
the DoubleConv block, we concatenate the upsampled feature map with the matching
encoder feature map via a skip connection. These skip connections are the key
innovation of U-Net — they restore fine-grained spatial details that would otherwise
be lost in the downsampling path.

Now here is where our adaptation comes in. In the original U-Net, the decoder ends
with a 1 × 1 convolution that produces a per-pixel class probability map. We do not
need that — we want a single class label per image. So instead of the segmentation
head, we attach an adaptive average pooling layer that collapses the entire spatial
feature map into a single 64-dimensional vector, and then a single fully connected
layer that maps those 64 features to 10 class scores.

This architecture has about 8.1 million parameters and weighs roughly 31 megabytes —
1.8 times more than SimpleCNN. But as we will see shortly, this cost is well justified.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 11 — Training procedure                                  [~1:30]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Both models are trained under identical conditions, which is important for a fair
comparison.

We use the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001. In our experiments, Adam
with this learning rate converged stably for both architectures. We also tried 0.01,
but that caused instability in the early training epochs — the loss oscillated
rather than decreasing smoothly.

The loss function is cross-entropy, which is the standard choice for multi-class
classification. The batch size is 64, which provides a good balance between gradient
noise and GPU memory usage. The maximum number of epochs is 50.

The key mechanism is early stopping. After each epoch, we compute the validation
loss. If it improved by more than a threshold called min_delta, we save the current
model weights as the best checkpoint and reset the patience counter. If there is no
sufficient improvement for a consecutive number of epochs — defined by the patience
hyperparameter, which we set to 5 — we stop training.

The min_delta is set to 0.001 for SimpleCNN and 0.0 for the U-Net. The reason for
the difference is that the U-Net's validation curve is smoother and benefits from
accepting even the smallest improvements, while the SimpleCNN's curve is noisier
and benefits from a small threshold to avoid false checkpoints.

Early stopping saved us 34 unnecessary epochs for SimpleCNN and 21 for the U-Net.
More importantly, it protects against overfitting — the model that gets evaluated
on test is the one that performed best on validation, not the last one to run.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 12 — Hyperparameter analysis                             [~1:15]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Let me briefly summarize the five hyperparameters that we found had the greatest
impact on the final result.

The most important one is the learning rate. Adam with 0.001 converged stably for
both models. When we tried 0.01, the training was unstable in the first few epochs,
and the models had difficulty recovering from early bad weight configurations.

The second is the Dropout rate of 0.5 in SimpleCNN's fully connected layer.
Without it, the validation loss started to diverge from the training loss after
about epoch 8, which is a classic sign of overfitting. With Dropout, this effect
was much milder.

The third is the patience of 5 epochs. We found this to be the right trade-off:
longer patience did not help because the model had already started to overfit by
that point; shorter patience stopped training before the model had a chance to
improve further.

The fourth is the batch size of 64. Smaller batches introduced too much gradient
noise, and larger batches consumed too much GPU memory without any meaningful
accuracy gain.

And the fifth — perhaps the most fundamental — is batch normalization. Removing it
from either model increased training time and reduced final accuracy by approximately
3 percentage points. It also made training more sensitive to the learning rate.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 13 — Results: SimpleCNN                                  [~1:45]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Let us now look at the results. We will start with SimpleCNN.

During training, SimpleCNN converged relatively quickly. The validation loss reached
its best value of 0.3105 at epoch 11, with a validation accuracy of 89.63% at that
point. Early stopping then triggered at epoch 16, after no significant improvement
was observed for five consecutive epochs. So the model trained for 16 epochs total
but the checkpoint saved at epoch 11 is what we evaluate.

On the held-out test set of 4,050 images, SimpleCNN achieved:
  • Accuracy: 89.23%
  • Weighted F1-score: 0.8930
  • Weighted Precision: 0.8963
  • Weighted Recall: 0.8923

These are solid numbers for a baseline model trained from scratch on just 18,900
images without any data augmentation.

Looking at the per-class results, the model performs best on classes that have
distinctive visual appearances. Forest achieves an F1 of 0.954 — trees from above
have a very recognizable texture. SeaLake reaches 0.948 — large water bodies look
very homogeneous and are easy to identify. Residential achieves 0.939 — the grid
pattern of streets and rooftops is a strong visual cue.

The hardest classes are HerbaceousVegetation at 0.821, PermanentCrop at 0.833, and
River at 0.836. These classes are visually ambiguous. Herbaceous vegetation and
pastures look almost identical at this resolution. Permanent crops — orchards and
vineyards — have a texture that overlaps with annual crops. And rivers at 10 meters
per pixel often appear as narrow strips that can be confused with highways or the
surrounding land.

You can see this reflected in the confusion matrix on the left — the main errors
are concentrated among these visually similar groups.

SLIDE 14 — Results: U-Net Classifier                           [~1:45]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Now the U-Net Classifier.

Training took longer to converge, as expected from a larger and more expressive
model. The best validation loss of 0.1116 was reached at epoch 24, with a validation
accuracy of 96.32% at that point. Early stopping triggered at epoch 29.

On the same test set, the U-Net Classifier achieved:
  • Accuracy: 96.10%
  • Weighted F1-score: 0.9610
  • Weighted Precision: 0.9613
  • Weighted Recall: 0.9610

This is a substantial improvement over SimpleCNN: almost 7 percentage points in
accuracy, and a similarly large improvement in all other metrics.

What is particularly noteworthy is that the improvement is not limited to the easy
classes — it is consistent across the board. Let us look at the classes that were
hardest for SimpleCNN.

River, which had an F1 of 0.836 in SimpleCNN, rises to 0.951 with U-Net — a gain
of 0.115 in a single architectural change. PermanentCrop goes from 0.833 to 0.940.
HerbaceousVegetation improves from 0.821 to 0.939.

Even the best classes get better: Residential goes from 0.939 to 0.986, SeaLake
from 0.948 to 0.979, Highway from 0.848 to 0.978.

The confusion matrix for U-Net, shown on the left, is visibly cleaner than the one
for SimpleCNN. The values are more concentrated along the main diagonal, and the
off-diagonal errors are smaller. This tells us that the architectural improvements
are systematic — they help everywhere, not just in a few categories.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 15 — Comparative analysis                                [~1:30]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Let us now compare both models side by side.

The table on this slide summarizes the key metrics and attributes of each model.

Test accuracy improves from 89.23% to 96.10% — a gain of 6.87 percentage points.
Weighted F1 improves from 0.8930 to 0.9610, a difference of 0.068. Best validation
loss drops from 0.3105 to 0.1116, which is a reduction of 64%.

SimpleCNN reached its best epoch at epoch 11, while U-Net required epoch 24 —
roughly twice as many epochs. SimpleCNN occupies 17.2 MB, U-Net occupies 30.9 MB —
about 1.8 times larger.

The worst-class F1 — which for both models is the River class — improves from
0.836 to 0.951, a gain of 0.115.

So what explains U-Net's superiority? We attribute it to three architectural factors.

First, skip connections allow the network to simultaneously access both high-level
abstract features from deep layers and low-level spatial details from shallow layers.
For classifying visually ambiguous land types — like distinguishing a crop field
from a pasture, or a river from a road — this dual access to detail and context is
extremely valuable.

Second, the DoubleConv blocks apply two convolutions per stage, effectively doubling
the receptive field at each resolution level compared to SimpleCNN's single convolution.

Third, the encoder hierarchy has four levels rather than three, which allows the
model to build more abstract and discriminative feature representations.

The cost — a model 1.8 times larger that takes twice as many epochs to train — is
clearly justified by the quality gain, especially in a use case where accuracy matters.

SLIDE 16 — Conclusions                                         [~1:15]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Let me now summarize the main conclusions of our project.

We designed, trained, and evaluated two deep learning architectures for land cover
classification on the EuroSAT dataset, using 27,000 Sentinel-2 RGB image patches
across 10 classes.

Our baseline model, SimpleCNN, achieves a test accuracy of 89.23% and a weighted
F1 of 0.893 after converging in just 11 epochs. This is a solid and practical
result for a lightweight model.

Our main model, U-Net Classifier, achieves 96.10% accuracy and a weighted F1 of
0.961. The improvement of 6.87 percentage points is consistent across all classes,
with the largest absolute gains on the previously hardest classes.

The central architectural lesson is that skip connections and hierarchical feature
reuse — the defining properties of U-Net — are highly effective for satellite image
classification. They allow the model to jointly exploit local textures and global
context, which is exactly what is needed to distinguish visually similar land types.

From an engineering standpoint, the use of a fixed random seed, a Docker container
with pinned dependencies, and a modular train.py → evaluate.py pipeline ensures
that all results are fully reproducible.

Practically, a model with 96% accuracy on 10 land cover classes can be directly
applied to smart-city monitoring, environmental change detection, and agricultural
mapping.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 17 — Future work                                         [~1:15]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Several clear directions remain open for future investigation.

The most promising single improvement would be to use all 13 Sentinel-2 spectral
bands rather than just RGB. The near-infrared and short-wave infrared channels carry
information that is not visible in ordinary color images — particularly for vegetation
health, soil moisture, and water depth. Using them would likely give a meaningful
boost to the classes that are currently hardest to separate.

Data augmentation is a straightforward next step. Random horizontal and vertical
flips, rotations, and small color jitter can significantly improve generalization,
especially for the under-represented classes like Pasture.

Transfer learning is another direction. Using a ResNet-50 or EfficientNet backbone
pre-trained on ImageNet as the U-Net encoder, and then fine-tuning on EuroSAT, could
potentially close most of the remaining gap to the 98% that Helber reported for
fine-tuned ResNet.

Temporal fusion is more ambitious but potentially very powerful. Combining images
from multiple dates at the same location would allow the model to capture seasonal
dynamics, which is critical for separating annual crops from permanent crops.

Finally, interpretability — applying Grad-CAM visualization to identify which regions
of the image drive a particular prediction — would make the model more trustworthy
and useful for planners and policymakers who need to understand why the system made
a certain decision.

APPENDIX — 10 Questions & Answers
(For reference only — not part of the spoken presentation)

Q1: Why do you use only RGB bands and not all 13 spectral bands of Sentinel-2?

A1: This is a deliberate simplification. The RGB version of EuroSAT is a widely used
standard benchmark, so our results are directly comparable with related published work.
We also wanted to isolate the contribution of the architecture itself, without mixing
in the effect of additional spectral information. Using all 13 bands is one of the
top priorities for our future work, and we expect it will particularly improve the
vegetation-related classes thanks to the NIR and SWIR channels.

------

Q2: Why does U-Net, designed for segmentation, work as a classifier?

A2: We adapted it by replacing the segmentation output head. In the classical U-Net,
the decoder ends with a 1×1 convolution that produces a per-pixel class map. We
replaced that with an AdaptiveAvgPool layer that collapses the spatial feature map
to a single 64-dimensional vector, followed by one fully connected layer to 10 classes.
The encoder, decoder, and skip connections remain unchanged — so the model still
benefits from multi-scale feature extraction, but it outputs one label per image
rather than a dense map.

------

Q3: Why do you select the best epoch by val_loss and not by val_accuracy?

A3: val_loss is a more sensitive and continuous metric. Accuracy is discrete — two
models can have identical accuracy while one is much more confident in its correct
predictions. Loss captures that confidence directly. In practice, validation loss
typically starts to degrade before validation accuracy does, which means we get an
earlier and more reliable signal that the model is beginning to overfit. Selecting by
val_loss therefore tends to produce more stable and better-calibrated checkpoints.

------

Q4: Why is there no data augmentation in the training loop?

A4: We deliberately excluded augmentation to get a clean comparison of the two
architectures under identical conditions, without introducing an extra variable that
complicates interpretation. Our goal was to measure what the architecture contributes,
not what preprocessing choices contribute. Augmentation is a straightforward next step,
and we expect it to help SimpleCNN proportionally more than U-Net because the baseline
model has more room to under-fit and less built-in structural robustness.

------

Q5: Which classes are confused most often and why?

A5: For SimpleCNN, the hardest classes are River (F1 = 0.836) and PermanentCrop
(F1 = 0.833). At 10 meters per pixel, a river often appears as a narrow strip that
looks similar to a highway or the fields on either side of it. PermanentCrop —
orchards and vineyards — shares texture with AnnualCrop and HerbaceousVegetation.
U-Net significantly reduces these errors — River rises to 0.951 and PermanentCrop
to 0.940 — because skip connections allow the network to combine fine spatial detail
with global context, which is exactly what you need to resolve these ambiguous cases.

------

Q6: U-Net is 1.8× larger and takes almost twice as long to train — is that justified?

A6: Absolutely. The accuracy gain is nearly 7 percentage points, the validation loss
drops by 64%, and the worst-class F1 improves by 0.115 in an absolute sense. These
are not marginal gains — they represent a qualitative improvement in the model's
ability to handle difficult classes. For inference, the extra 13 MB is negligible.
Training is a one-time cost. In any application where accuracy matters — such as
environmental monitoring or urban planning — this trade-off is clearly worth it.

------

Q7: How do you guard against data leakage between train, val, and test?

A7: We use torch.random_split with a fixed seed of 42. This ensures that every run
of train.py and evaluate.py operates on exactly the same partition of the data. The
test set is never used for any decision during training — not for hyperparameter
selection, not for checkpoint saving, not for early stopping. The validation set
handles all of that. The test set is touched exactly once, at the end, to report
the final metrics. This guarantees that our reported numbers reflect true
generalization to previously unseen examples.

------

Q8: Why BatchNorm rather than LayerNorm or GroupNorm?

A8: Batch Normalization is the standard, well-studied choice for convolutional networks
with medium-sized batches — we use 64, which is comfortably in the regime where BN
works well. It normalizes activations across the batch dimension, which reduces
internal covariate shift and allows higher learning rates. In our preliminary
experiments, removing BN reduced accuracy by about 3 percentage points and required
a lower learning rate to avoid divergence. LayerNorm and GroupNorm would be better
choices for very small batches or in transformer architectures, but for our setup
BN is both simpler and more effective.

------

Q9: Why is F1 computed with 'weighted' averaging?

A9: Weighted averaging weights each class's F1 score by the number of samples it
has in the test set. This correctly reflects the real class distribution: we have
2,000 Pasture samples but 3,000 Forest samples. If we used macro averaging, every
class would count equally regardless of how many examples it has, which would
over-represent the rare classes and give a misleading picture of overall performance.
Weighted F1 more accurately represents how the model would behave in a real
deployment scenario where the class frequencies match those in the dataset.

------

Q10: How do you ensure reproducibility of the experiments?

A10: Through four mechanisms. First, torch.Generator().manual_seed(42) in
random_split ensures the same data partition on every run. Second, pipeline.py
launches train.py and evaluate.py using sys.executable — the same Python interpreter
as the calling process — which avoids environment mismatches between different
virtual environments. Third, the Dockerfile pins the Python version and all
dependencies via requirements.txt, so the environment is reproducible on any machine.
Fourth, all hyperparameters and training outcomes are saved to JSON files in the
results/ directory — results/<model>_training_summary.json stores the best epoch,
best val_loss, and stopping conditions, while results/<model>_metrics.json stores
the final test metrics. Together these four measures mean that any run of the
pipeline can be fully reconstructed and verified.
